30-second TL;DR

Two official data exports — DoorDash (852 orders) and Uber Eats (643 orders) — were deduplicated from 3,000+ raw line-items into 1,495 real orders spanning 2017–2026, almost entirely in Orlando. Together they total roughly $28,600 in food subtotals (fees, tax, and tips aren't in the exports, so true spend is higher). A third file — Uber rides — is small but tells its own story.

The combined picture is remarkably consistent: a few loyal restaurants, a daytime delivery rhythm, and order sizes that have roughly tripled as the habit matured from "$7 fast-food runs" into "$25 proper meals."

Core insight: Food delivery is a lunch-and-breakfast work-fuel habit, not a late-night vice — Chick-fil-A and 4 Rivers anchor a fiercely local Orlando rotation — and the Uber app is basically an airport shuttle (48% of rides).

Average order value by year, combined. The pandemic fast-food era dragged the average down to ~$12; since 2023 it's been climbing toward $30 as cheap McDonald's runs gave way to real meals.

Looking at DoorDash alone, the climb is even starker: its average order went from ~$7 in 2020–21 to ~$27 in 2025 — a near-quadrupling as the pandemic snack-runs gave way to sit-down meals.

What Kevin likes to eat — the flavor profile

Classifying all 1,495 orders by cuisine reveals a clear taste fingerprint: chicken-forward, comfort-leaning, and fiercely repetitive, with a strong Mexican streak and a wide Asian range. Three buckets — chicken, Asian, and Mexican — are nearly half of everything ordered.

The one-liner: Kevin defaults to fast-casual over fine dining, eats the same handful of dishes over and over, and is savory and protein-driven, not sweet — his indulgence is fried chicken and BBQ, not dessert.

The synthesis

Proteins: heavily chicken-forward — fast-food chicken is the #1 bucket and #1 dish, and chicken shows up inside every other cuisine (sesame chicken, fried-chicken rice bowls, Greek chicken pita, Bonchon). Beef via burgers and BBQ brisket; pork via Cuban, ramen, and tacos.

Spice: mild-to-medium. Plenty of tacos, salsa, and carne asada (Mexican is 15%), but little explicit Thai/Indian/Szechuan — his "spicy" is Tex-Mex, not chili-forward.

Comfort vs healthy: comfort wins decisively — fried chicken, burgers, BBQ, diner breakfast, Cuban. The healthy/bowls lane (Fresh Kitchen, CAVA, Just Salad, Bolay, poke) is real but secondary (~4%).

Sweet tooth: essentially none on delivery — 12 dessert/boba orders in nine years. He indulges in fried chicken, not sugar.

How the palate matured

2017–19: subs, Chinese, and burgers — a younger, cheaper, fast-food palate.

2020–21 (peak, ~710 orders): dominated by fast-food chicken and Mexican — quick, cheap, repeatable comfort runs (~$7 orders).

2022–24: broadened — ramen and healthy bowls climbed, AOV roughly tripled toward ~$25–30 as snack-runs became real meals.

2025–26: lower-volume, more varied — pizza, Mediterranean, and sit-down American rising. Fewer, nicer, more deliberate orders.

Chicken in every cuisine. Chick-n-Strips, sesame chicken, Greek chicken pita, fried-chicken rice bowls, Bonchon, Huey Magoo's — no matter the menu, Kevin finds the chicken.
Weekday chicken, weekend diner. Chicken and Mexican over-index hard on weekday lunches (work fuel); breakfast/diner and pub food skew toward weekends (Metro Diner, First Watch, Gator's Dockside).
Adventurous across, repetitive within. He'll try Cuban, ramen, Malaysian (Mamak), and Argentine (Alma Argentina) — but then orders the same dish at each, dozens of times.
One party-order outlier. Tacos Chely 5y10: just 3 orders but ~$688 — huge group orders, not a personal habit. A reminder that spend and taste aren't the same signal.

Things the data quietly knew

Chick-fil-A is the whole personality. 209 combined orders — more than McDonald's, Chipotle, Metro Diner and Panda Express put together. Chick-n-Strips ordered 94 separate times.
There is essentially no late-night delivery. 17 orders after 10pm in 9 years (~1%). The "55% late-night" you'd see in the raw data is a pure UTC artifact — corrected, it's a 9-to-5 habit.
The two apps quietly split the day. Uber Eats handles breakfast and lunch; DoorDash owns dinner (493 of its orders at 6–7pm). Same person, two different mealtime tools.
Order value nearly quadrupled. ~$7 DoorDash orders in the 2020–21 fast-food era climbed to ~$27 by 2025 — the habit grew from snack-runs into actual meals.
Half of all Uber rides are airport runs — and the 13-city spread (Cairo, Taipei, Seoul, Rome, Bogotá…) doubles as a corroboration of every major trip taken.
The delivery addresses are a move log. Three Orlando homes, three date ranges, no calendar required — the food just followed Kevin around.

Honest caveats

Spend is understated. Both exports give item subtotals only — no delivery fees, service fees, tax, or tips. Real out-of-pocket is meaningfully higher than the ~$28.6K shown, and tip behavior can't be computed at all.
Uber rides are capped at 100 rows. Uber's export keeps only the most recent ~100 trips, so the 87 completed rides are a recent sample (2019–2025), not a lifetime count — the account dates to 2015 in Gainesville.
Timezones were UTC despite a "local" label. All meal-time figures were converted to America/New_York (Orlando). Orders placed while traveling in other timezones may be off by a few hours.
AOV is a floor, not a ceiling. Many DoorDash orders show only a single line-item, which likely undercounts real cart contents and depresses the average — treat AOV as conservative.
Cuisine mix is heuristic. Restaurants were classified into cuisines by name-keyword matching, so ~4% land in "Other" and a few fusion/gastropub spots are judgment calls — treat the percentages as directional, not exact.
Modifiers inflate item counts. Add-ons (a guac side, "Garlic Rice") read as their own rows, so high-ranking sides like Waffle Fries and Guacamole partly reflect that they ride along with nearly every order at their restaurant — not standalone cravings.

Request your exports
DoorDash: account → data export. Uber: privacy.uber.com → download your data (Eats + rides come together).
Dedupe line-items into orders
Load each CSV in pandas, group by the order timestamp, sum the subtotals. That's the single most important step.
Convert UTC → your local timezone
pd.to_datetime(..., utc=True).dt.tz_convert('America/New_York') before doing any meal-time analysis.
Normalize names, then count
Merge "Chick-fil-A" / "Chick-fil-A®", strip "(Orlando)" suffixes, convert ride fares to one currency. Then chart frequency, spend, by-year, by-hour, and address.
