30-second TL;DR

I parsed my raw Netflix export — 844 timestamped plays from May 22, 2020 to Mar 22, 2026 — and broke it down by show, format, year, weekday, and month. The verdict: I'm a completionist binger with a laser focus on heists, con artists, crime, and spy thrillers. Core finding: I don't sample — I commit. 31 different series got 5+ episodes, led by full-series runs of White Collar (70), Better Call Saul (63), and Money Heist (48). And the volume isn't steady: 2024 alone was 287 plays — more than double any other year.

How it works + a war story

The clever bit is the title parser. Netflix stores episodes as "Show: Season N: Episode" but films are just "Title" — and plenty of films have colons too (Jack Reacher: Never Go Back). The fix: only treat a row as an episode when it contains a season/part/episode/chapter keyword, then roll up to the text before the first colon.

War story: mid-analysis the working environment started returning garbled and duplicated output — even stray text that didn't belong. Rather than trust a flaky readout, every figure here was re-derived from a single structured data file and cross-checked three ways: the by-year, by-weekday, and by-month breakdowns each independently sum to exactly 844. Trust the source and verify the totals — not the screen.
