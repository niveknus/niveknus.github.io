Things Worth Noticing

Someone plagiarized a top comment, in the same thread. #4 (@antarikshwahi, 2,746 ♥) wrote "Rain still drops on a man standing still, better just move through the storm." Further down, @tristanmanisone posted the same line reworded with a wall of fire emoji, and pulled 5 likes. The internet noticed.
The most self-aware entry cracked the top 12. @sachin.5.__ replied to a motivational-quote thread with "quotes don't work, until you work" and got 434 likes for it.
One comment beat the algorithm and the rules. A comment with 272 likes came back completely blank — deleted or hidden by Instagram, but its likes survived. It's excluded here since there's nothing left to read.
Only 160 of the 900 comments scanned got a single like. That's about 18%. Posting a quote here was, for almost everyone, shouting into a void.

Method & Caveats

How these were pulled, and what "top" means

Comments were fetched through Instagram's own private API using an authenticated session, which returns comments in Instagram's ranked order (highest-engagement first) along with true like counts.

Scanned 900 of 5,663 comments, not all of them. That's deliberate: because Instagram front-loads its ranking, the liked comments cluster at the top. Past rank 300 in that ordering, no comment scanned exceeded 6 likes — so the top tier is fully captured and deeper paging would only add more zero-like noise.

Floor for inclusion: 3 likes. Below that, 110 comments had 1-2 likes and 740 had none.

A first attempt via a third-party scraper (Apify) returned every comment with a like count of 0 or 1 — its comment like counts are simply broken. Worth knowing if this ever gets automated.

Quotes are reproduced verbatim, including original spelling and punctuation. Wrapping quotation marks were stripped for typographic consistency; nothing else was edited.
