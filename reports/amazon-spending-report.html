30-second TL;DR

This is a revealed-spending profile — not what someone says they care about, but what they actually paid for, line by line, for sixteen-plus years. The export covers 2,990 retail line items across 1,597 orders, Dec 2009 → May 2026, totaling ~$95,300 in retail spend (plus a separate ~$6,900 in digital/Audible).

Spending was thin through the college years (under ~$3.5K/yr), then detonated in 2016 ($13,162) and 2017 ($11,332) — the Prime-era, first-real-job buying spike — before settling into a steady $5K–$8K/yr rhythm that holds to today.

Core insight: the receipts don't lie. This is a gadget-forward, hobby-cycling, DIY-capable household optimizer who buys deep into each new interest, maintains his own car and home, and keeps the boring consumables on repeat.

The clever bits (and the honest snags)

🐛 Two return files that disagreed

The export ships returns in more than one place. A small Returns.1 file listed only 8 rows; the real Returns Status.csv held 89. Easy to under-count if you grab the first file you find — the report uses the complete 89-record set ($6,767 refunded).

🧹 "Closed" vs "Cancelled"

3,003 raw rows include 13 Cancelled lines that were never paid for. Counting them would have inflated both the item count and spend, so they're dropped — leaving 2,990 real purchases.

🏷️ Categories are best-effort, not gospel

There's no category field in the export, so categories are inferred by keyword-matching product names. The big buckets (electronics, home, tools) are solid; a long tail of one-off items lands in "misc." The year/spend timeline, big-ticket list, repeat-buys, and returns are exact — those come straight from the columns.

Things the receipts quietly knew

A $585 ChargePoint Level-2 EV charger bought in 2024 is a dead giveaway: you don't hardwire a home fast-charger unless there's an electric car in the garage. The data knew about the EV before any "I bought an EV" note did.
The Jocko GO energy drink habit is real — 38 orders (~$1,246) clustered tightly between mid-2021 and early-2023. A whole caffeine era with a clear beginning and end.
The 2016 spike wasn't lifestyle creep — it was a PC build, a phone, a TV, and a full optics setup all landing in one year. First-real-job money meeting a backlog of wants.
Rifle optics is the highest average-price category in the entire export — only ~10 items, but they're expensive items. When this buyer commits, he buys the good glass.
Pickleball shows the classic new-hobby arc: a starter paddle in 2023, then the $298 pro model in 2025 once it stuck. You can watch an interest harden into a commitment.
Mann oil and air filters bought on repeat is a quiet tell of a DIY car owner — nobody buys their own OEM filters unless they're turning the wrench themselves.

Do this yourself

Request your Amazon data
amazon.com → Account → Request Your Information → "Your Orders." Arrives as a zip in a day or two.
Load Order History.csv with pandas
Filter to Order Status == "Closed", parse Order Date, coerce Total Amount to numeric.
Group by year and by ASIN
Year groups give the timeline; ASIN groups with count ≥ 4 surface your recurring buys instantly.
Keyword-classify product names
A dictionary of substrings → categories does 80% of the work. Eyeball the "misc" pile and add rules.
Render it in one HTML file
Bars are just divs with a percentage width. No chart library — opens offline, shares as a single file.
