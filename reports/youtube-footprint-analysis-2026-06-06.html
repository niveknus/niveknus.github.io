The clever bits (and the honest snags)

The data is thin on purpose — so don't fake depth

The temptation with a YouTube export is to talk about viewing habits. There are none here: history was off, so the watch-history.html file contains exactly two rows. Rather than stretch two videos into a "viewing analysis," the report leans entirely on the durable signals — subscriptions and playlists — and says plainly that history is empty. Honest beats impressive.

🐛 The undated five

Of 55 Watch Later videos, only 50 have a creation timestamp; 5 export with a blank date field (likely added before YouTube started stamping playlist items, or migrated). They're counted separately rather than silently dropped or guessed — the year chart shows them as a grey "undated" bar so the 55 still reconciles.

One comment, nine chat lines, and that's the whole social trail

The comments file has a single entry (2024). The live-chats file has ~9 lines, all from one creator's 2021–2022 streams ("!play", "RIOT" — typical Twitch/YouTube chat spam). Kevin watches and saves; he almost never posts. The footprint is a reader's, not a poster's.

Things the data quietly knew

The only two videos in the entire watch history — logged the day history flipped on, May 30, 2026 — are "Learn 97% of Claude in Under 16 Minutes" and "How I Use Claude Cowork to Automate 99% Of My Life." The first thing the history ever recorded was Kevin learning to automate his life with Claude. You're reading the result.
There's a channel subscription for one specific car (The Ioniq Guy) — and a matching EV route-planner app in the Google Pay data. The export agrees with itself across services.
A "Fitness" playlist (3 videos, 2023) and an "Inspiration" playlist (1 video, Mar 2026) exist alongside Watch Later — tiny, but they're the only two themed lists he bothered to make. Both are functional, both private.
The Watch Later list predates four phone upgrades (the account cycles near-annual Samsung Galaxy flagships) and still survives — a five-year throughline in an otherwise churny digital life.

Do this yourself

Reproducing this from a Google Takeout YouTube export:

  1. Export YouTube and YouTube Music from takeout.google.com (CSV format for playlists/subscriptions).
  2. Read subscriptions/subscriptions.csv — group the channel titles into a handful of themes by hand; count per theme for the bar chart.
  3. For each playlists/*-videos.csv, take column 2 (creation timestamp), slice to YYYY and YYYY-MM, and sort | uniq -c for the year and month histograms.
  4. Check history/watch-history.html for actual viewing — if it's near-empty, history was off; lead with subscriptions instead.
  5. Render it all in one self-contained HTML file with hand-built CSS bars and an inline-SVG sparkline. No libraries.
