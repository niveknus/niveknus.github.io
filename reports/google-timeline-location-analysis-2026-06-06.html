30-second TL;DR

This is a near-complete movement log for the spring of 2026, reconstructed from 1,325 semantic segments and 49,671 raw sensor signals (Wi-Fi scans, GPS positions, activity records). Across 79 days, the picture is sharp and a little funny: a deeply car-centric life — 360 separate drives, each ending in a logged parking event — orbiting a tight home/work loop barely a mile and a half apart. Then, in the middle of it, a single round-trip flight to San Diego that travels farther than every drive, walk, bike ride, and run of the entire season combined.

Core insight: 360 drives built daily life; one flight (2,161 mi) doubled the distance. Routine is local and dense; the mileage hides in the rare long jump.

The honest caveat: this is a snapshot, not a life

One thing worth saying plainly: this is Google's new on-device Timeline format, which replaced the old cloud-stored Location History in 2024. Instead of years of movement, it keeps only a recent rolling window — here, exactly 2.5 months (Mar 13 → May 30). So this isn't a life history; it's a clean, dense core sample of one spring.

The upside: because it's the live on-device log, the fidelity is excellent — 49,671 raw signals across 79 days, every drive caught, every parking event stamped. The data we do have is trustworthy down to the minute; there's just less of it. Coordinates for non-home/work places are also anonymized in the export, so nothing here was geocoded or looked up externally — every figure comes straight from the lat/lng, timestamps, and Google's own HOME/WORK labels in the file.

Things the map quietly knew

360 drives, 360 parkings. A perfect one-to-one — every single trip ended with a logged park. The car is never just left running; it's a life lived between parking spots.
The commute is walkable and mostly isn't. Home and work are ~1.5 mi apart, yet the car still wins ~62% of the time on that hop. Convenience beats distance.
8:15 AM is the tell. Google identified the home→work leg as a recurring trip that reliably fires around minute 495 of the day — a quiet fingerprint of a routine.
One Tuesday rewrote the whole dataset. May 5's flight (2,162 mi) is larger than the other 78 days of driving, walking, cycling, and running put together.
Walking and running barely register. 12 miles walked, 3 run — combined, less than a single average day's drive. The 42 cycling miles across 6 rides actually out-distance both.
Steady as a metronome. Outside the flight week, weekly mileage never strayed far from ~110–210 mi. This is a life with a very consistent radius.
