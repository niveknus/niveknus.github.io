The bottom line first

If I'm managing your money, this is the first place I'd point. Your Amazon spending is mostly capex — things you keep. Food delivery is the opposite: every dollar is gone the same day, and you're paying a second tax on top of the food — delivery fees, service fees, tips, and inflated in-app menu prices — purely to avoid a 10-minute drive. The food itself isn't the problem (it's the stuff you love). The delivery wrapper is.

The key reframe: the export shows $28,633 in food subtotals — but that number excludes every fee and tip. Add the real cost of delivery and you've spent closer to $40K+, meaning roughly $11–17K of pure convenience premium over the years. That premium is the cleanest "found money" in your whole financial picture — and you've already proven you can claw it back.

The hidden number: what delivery actually costs

This is the single most important thing to internalize. The data only records the food subtotal. But every delivery order also carries a stack of charges the export doesn't show:

So the real lifetime number isn't $28.6K — it's ~$40–46K (estimated). On your small orders the math is even worse: a $12 order with $9 of fees+tip is a ~75% surcharge. That's why the high-frequency era below was so expensive per dollar of actual food. (Fee figures are industry-typical estimates — the exact charges aren't in Amazon/Uber's export. Treat the range as directional, not exact.)

Waste vs. value — the convenience-tax ledger

✓ Fair value
  • The food itself — fast-casual, ~$13–30/order; this is reasonable eating, not extravagance
  • Genuine convenience moments — sick, slammed, no car; delivery earns its fee when the alternative is real
  • The 2025 discipline — you already right-sized frequency; that's the model
⚠ Pure premium / leak
  • Delivery + service fees — ~$5–10/order to skip a 10-min drive on food you'd happily pick up
  • Tips on top of fees — fair to the driver, but it's cost you control by picking up
  • In-app menu markup — you pay more per item than walking in, invisibly
  • Small-order delivery — the worst ratio; fees can be ~75% of a $12 order
  • Default-to-delivery on go-to spots — CFA, Chipotle, 4 Rivers pickup is trivial

The plan — keep the food, cut the wrapper

Make pickup the default, delivery the exception
Same food, but you drop the delivery fee, the service fee, and most of the tip — often 40%+ off the all-in price. For your anchors (Chick-fil-A, 4 Rivers, Chipotle, Little Greek) pickup is a 10-minute round trip. Reserve delivery for genuine can't-leave moments.
Batch-cook your own greatest hits
Your flavor profile is a short, repeatable list — chicken, Mexican, bowls. Cook the 3–4 staples you reorder most on a Sunday; you'll out-eat delivery at a fraction of the cost and barely change what you're eating.
Kill small-order delivery entirely
Set a hard floor: if the food is under ~$20, never pay to deliver it — pick it up or skip it. Small orders are where the fee-to-food ratio is most punishing.
Set a monthly delivery cap and watch the 2026 creep
2025 ran ~$120/month in food subtotal — a great target. 2026 is trending higher; a simple monthly cap (and a glance at the running total) keeps the win you already earned.
Auto-sweep the difference to savings
Hold delivery at 2025 levels and the ~$4K/yr you're not spending vs. 2024 should land in savings automatically, not drift back into checking. This is real progress on your save-more goal (G2).
The number that matters: holding delivery at 2025's ~$1.5K instead of 2024's ~$5.5K is ~$4,000/yr of food subtotal — and well over $6,000 all-in once fees and tips are counted. Stack that on the ~$2,500/yr from the Amazon trimming, and you're most of the way to a serious savings year without touching rent, car, or income.

One honest caveat

Two limits to keep in mind. (1) The fee/tip/markup figures are industry-typical estimates — the exports record only food subtotals, so the "~$40K all-in" and "~40–60% premium" are directional, not your exact charges (your real receipts would confirm them). (2) This still only covers delivery — it says nothing about restaurants and bars you pay for in person, which for most people is larger. The full picture needs a bank/credit-card statement analysis (your dining-out total, plus subscriptions and the rest) — the same next step flagged in your Amazon analysis.
